# Reading Notes: *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* — Kuhn (1962)

Second reading. My first was in an undergraduate survey course where the only takeaway was "paradigm shift = big change." That framing flattens everything interesting.

## What I missed the first time

Chapters 6–8 are doing much more careful work than the popular reception suggests. Kuhn is not saying that paradigm shifts are *arbitrary* — he's arguing that the criteria by which we evaluate theories are themselves internal to paradigms, which creates a genuine incommensurability problem without collapsing into relativism.

> "The transfer of allegiance from paradigm to paradigm is a conversion experience that cannot be forced."

This is a psychological claim as much as a philosophical one. It sits uncomfortably with the normative picture of science as rational inquiry — but Kuhn isn't denying rationality, he's questioning whether "rationality" is paradigm-independent.

## Key distinctions I want to track

| Term | Kuhn's meaning | Common misreading |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| Paradigm | Disciplinary matrix + exemplars | "Any framework" |
| Normal science | Puzzle-solving within a paradigm | "Routine/boring" |
| Anomaly | Persistent failure of expected puzzle solution | "Any surprising result" |
| Revolution | Paradigm replacement, not mere theory swap | "Big discovery" |

## Open questions

1. Is incommensurability between paradigms *total* or *partial*? Kuhn seems to waver. Later work (especially "Commensurability, Comparability, Communicability") tries to nuance this.
2. How does the exemplar notion of paradigm relate to Wittgenstein's family resemblance? There's clearly a connection but Kuhn doesn't develop it.
3. What's the unit of a revolution? Kuhn uses Copernicus, Lavoisier, Einstein — but these span very different scales.
